# M&A / Acquisition

## When this applies
"Should we acquire company X," "is this a good deal," "how should we
structure/value this acquisition," "should we sell."

## Core questions, in order
1. **Strategic rationale** — why this target, why now? (market entry,
   capability/tech acquisition, consolidation/scale, vertical integration,
   talent/acquihire, removing a competitor). The rationale determines what
   "success" even means for the deal.
2. **Standalone value** — what is the target worth on its own, independent
   of the buyer? (valuation multiples vs. comparable transactions/companies,
   DCF sanity check, or simple multiple of revenue/EBITDA if data is thin).
3. **Synergies** — what is the deal worth *because of* the combination?
   - Revenue synergies (cross-sell, new channels, pricing power) — usually
     smaller and slower to realize than initially modeled; discount them.
   - Cost synergies (overhead consolidation, procurement scale, facility
     consolidation) — usually the more reliable and faster-realized bucket.
   - Dis-synergies (customer attrition from the merger itself, key talent
     leaving, integration distraction cost) — frequently omitted; don't
     omit them here.
4. **Price the deal**: standalone value + synergies - integration cost -
   dis-synergies, compared against the ask price / premium. State the
   maximum price that still creates value, not just whether the asking
   price is "good."
5. **Integration risk and feasibility** — culture fit, systems/process
   integration complexity, regulatory/antitrust risk, retention risk for
   key people. A deal that pencils out financially can still fail on
   execution risk — say so if it's material.
6. **Alternatives** — what's the counterfactual? Build organically, partner/
   JV instead of buy, acquire a different target, or do nothing. A deal
   should be compared against the next-best alternative, not just "buy vs.
   don't."

## Common traps
- Valuing synergies at the buyer's wishful-thinking case instead of a
  risk-adjusted one.
- Ignoring integration cost and dis-synergies entirely.
- Treating "strategic fit" as a justification that doesn't need a number
  attached.

## Typical recommendation shapes
Proceed at or below a stated max price, proceed with specific integration
conditions, renegotiate structure (earnout, staged acquisition), or walk
away in favor of a named alternative.
