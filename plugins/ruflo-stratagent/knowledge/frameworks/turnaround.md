# Turnaround

## When this applies
"The business is in serious decline/distress," "we need to turn this
around," often combining profitability decline + cost reduction +
potentially divestiture, under time pressure and sometimes liquidity
constraints.

## Core sequence (stabilize, then fix, then grow — in that order)
1. **Triage for survival first.** Is there a near-term liquidity/cash
   problem that must be solved before any strategic fix matters? If so,
   that's the actual first answer (cash management, covenant/debt
   renegotiation, emergency cost actions) — don't lead with a strategic
   repositioning recommendation while the company might run out of cash
   first.
2. **Diagnose root cause** using the profitability.md tree (price/volume/
   mix/cost bridge) plus: is this a market-wide decline (industry in
   structural decline — different answer) or a company-specific share/
   execution problem (fixable)?
3. **Segment the portfolio.** Which parts of the business are fundamentally
   viable (fix and keep) vs. structurally challenged (divest, shut down,
   or deprioritize)? Don't try to fix everything uniformly — concentrate
   effort on what's savable.
4. **Stabilize**: quick-win cost actions (see cost-reduction.md) and any
   revenue actions with fast payback, to stop the bleeding and buy time.
5. **Fix**: structural changes to the viable core — cost structure,
   pricing, portfolio focus.
6. **Grow**: only once stabilized and fixed, return to growth.md for
   forward-looking strategy. Recommending growth initiatives before
   stabilization is a common and serious mistake in turnaround cases.

## Common traps
- Proposing growth or strategic investment before confirming the company
  has the cash/time to survive to execute it.
- Treating all business units uniformly instead of segmenting viable vs.
  not.
- Underestimating the organizational/morale cost of turnaround actions on
  execution risk for the rest of the plan.

## Typical recommendation shapes
A phased plan explicitly sequenced stabilize → fix → grow, with the
viability segmentation of the portfolio stated up front.
