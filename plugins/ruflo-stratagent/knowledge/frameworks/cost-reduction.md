# Cost reduction

## When this applies
"We need to cut costs by X%," "reduce our cost base," "improve efficiency"
— distinct from profitability decline in that the mandate to cut is often
already given; the question is where and how, without breaking the business.

## Core tree
```
Cost = Fixed + Variable
     = COGS + SG&A (+ Capex/depreciation if relevant)
     = Direct + Allocated/overhead
```

## Diagnostic sequence
1. **Decompose the cost base** along the tree above before naming any
   target — you cannot prioritize cuts you haven't categorized.
2. **Benchmark** each major bucket against industry norms or best-in-class
   peers where data exists, to find where the company is structurally out
   of line vs. where it's already efficient.
3. **Classify each lever by type**:
   - Quick wins (procurement renegotiation, discretionary spend, low risk,
     fast payback).
   - Structural (headcount/org redesign, facility consolidation, process
     redesign — higher savings, slower, higher one-time cost and risk).
   - Strategic (exit a product line/segment/geography entirely — largest
     savings, highest risk, may also cut revenue).
4. **Always pair a savings estimate with implementation cost and time** —
   never present a run-rate saving without the cost/time to get there.
5. **Check second-order effects** explicitly: which cuts risk revenue,
   quality, morale/retention, or capability needed for future growth. A
   cost program that hits its savings target but breaks something load-
   bearing is not a win.

## Common traps
- Across-the-board percentage cuts instead of targeted, benchmarked cuts.
- Quoting savings without implementation cost, making the payback look
  better than it is.
- Cutting capability that's needed for a growth initiative running in
  parallel.

## Typical recommendation shapes
A prioritized list of levers (quick win / structural / strategic) with
savings, cost-to-implement, timeline, and flagged risk for each, sequenced
so quick wins fund or precede the structural work.
