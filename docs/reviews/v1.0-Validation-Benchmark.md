# StratAgent v1.0 — Validation Benchmark & Scoring Rubric

**Purpose:** Independent QA benchmark for the v1.0 readiness campaign.
**Author:** Independent Consulting QA (evaluation only — not implementation).
**Date:** 2026-07-09

This file defines (1) the 30-case consulting benchmark dataset and (2) the
13-criterion scoring rubric. Cases are fictional but realistic; each is a
self-contained brief of the kind an end user pastes into `/solve-case`.
No case contains a pre-worked solution — that is the system's job to produce.

### C01 — Profitability · Mid-market · Manufacturing
**Client:** Meridian Fasteners (industrial fasteners, $180M revenue, 620 staff).
Operating margin fell from 21% to 9% over two years. Management blames "the market."
Volume is flat, list prices unchanged, but realized prices are down and scrap rates
rose from 3% to 7%. A new ERP went live 18 months ago. The CEO wants to know whether
this is a pricing problem, an operations problem, or both, and what to fix first.

### C02 — Profitability · Enterprise · Healthcare
**Client:** St. Aldwyn Health System (12 hospitals, $2.1B net patient revenue).
EBITDA margin compressed from 6% to 1.5% in 18 months. Labor cost per adjusted
discharge is up 19%; agency-nurse spend tripled. Payer mix shifted 4pp toward
Medicaid. The board wants a recovery plan that does not compromise clinical quality
scores, which are tied to reimbursement.

### C03 — Profitability · Small · Consumer goods
**Client:** Brightleaf Tea Co. (DTC + wholesale, $14M revenue, 40 staff).
Gross margin is healthy at 62% but the company is barely breakeven. CAC has risen
from $18 to $41 in two years while AOV is flat at $34. Wholesale is 30% of revenue at
half the contribution margin of DTC. Founder wants to know if the business model still
works and where the profit leak is.

### C04 — Market Entry · Enterprise · Technology
**Client:** Northwind Cloud (US enterprise data-platform vendor, $900M ARR).
Considering entry into the EU with a Frankfurt region launch. GDPR/data-residency
demand is real, but three entrenched competitors hold ~70% share and a hyperscaler
bundles a competing product. Question: enter directly, partner with a local SI, or
acquire a small regional player — and is the EU worth the distraction from a growing
APAC opportunity?

### C05 — Market Entry · Mid-market · Consumer goods
**Client:** Casa Verde (Mexican premium-mezcal brand, $55M revenue).
Wants to enter the US market. Three-tier distribution, high slotting fees, and a
crowded premium-agave category. They have $8M to invest and one celebrity investor.
Decision: on-premise-first (bars/restaurants) vs. retail-first vs. DTC-e-commerce,
and which 3 states to seed.

### C06 — Market Entry · Small · Financial services
**Client:** Kettle (UK fintech, consumer savings app, 90k users, pre-Series A).
Considering expanding from savings into "buy now, pay later." Regulatory scrutiny of
BNPL is tightening. The team has payments infrastructure but no credit-underwriting
capability. Decision: build BNPL, partner with an existing BNPL provider, or stay
focused on savings and add investing instead.

### C07 — Pricing Strategy · Enterprise · Technology
**Client:** Palisade Analytics (B2B SaaS, $400M ARR, seat-based pricing).
Net revenue retention slipped from 118% to 104%. Usage has decoupled from seats —
customers automate workflows and reduce seat counts. A competitor launched
consumption-based pricing. Decision: migrate to usage-based pricing, hybrid, or
defend seat-based — without triggering a churn spike in the installed base.

### C08 — Pricing Strategy · Mid-market · Manufacturing
**Client:** TorqueLine (industrial pumps, $220M revenue, 1,100 SKUs).
Pricing is cost-plus and hasn't been reviewed in years. Discounting is delegated to
sales with no floor; realized margin varies 15–48% across identical products. Raw
material costs rose 20%. Decision: introduce value/segmented pricing and a discount
governance framework — and quantify the margin at stake.

### C09 — Cost Reduction · Enterprise · Manufacturing
**Client:** Halberd Automotive Components (Tier-1 supplier, $3.4B revenue).
An OEM customer (28% of revenue) demanded a 12% price cut over three years. Halberd
must remove $250M of cost without losing the contract. Labor is unionized; two of
seven plants are high-cost. Decision: where to cut (footprint, procurement, labor
productivity, SG&A) and in what sequence to protect the customer relationship.

### C10 — Cost Reduction · Public sector · Government
**Client:** State Department of Motor Services (14M residents, $480M annual budget).
A legislative mandate requires a 10% budget cut while improving service (wait times
average 90 minutes). Headcount is 70% of cost; union rules limit layoffs. Decision:
which services to digitize, which offices to consolidate, and how to sequence cuts
without breaching the service mandate.

### C11 — Growth Strategy · Mid-market · Technology
**Client:** Loopwork (project-management SaaS, $60M ARR, growth slowing 40%→18%).
Core PM market is saturating. Options on the table: (a) move upmarket to enterprise,
(b) add adjacent modules (docs, CRM) to grow ACV, (c) expand internationally, (d)
build a platform/marketplace. Board wants a prioritized growth thesis with the
sequencing and the bet that has the best risk-adjusted return.

### C12 — Growth Strategy · Enterprise · Financial services
**Client:** Cordillera Bank (regional US bank, $40B assets).
Deposit growth stalled; younger customers bank elsewhere. Options: acquire a
neobank, build a digital-only brand, deepen wallet share with existing customers via
wealth/insurance cross-sell, or expand into two adjacent states. Regulator relations
are sensitive. Decision: the highest-return growth vector given capital constraints.

### C13 — M&A · Enterprise · Consumer goods
**Client:** Summit Brands (packaged-foods conglomerate, $6B revenue).
Evaluating acquisition of GreenFork (plant-based foods, $400M revenue, growing 35%,
EBITDA-negative). Asking price is 6× revenue. Summit's core is declining 2%/year.
Decision: is GreenFork worth $2.4B, what synergies are real vs. hoped-for, and what
would have to be true for the deal to create value?

### C14 — M&A · Mid-market · Technology
**Client:** Vector Labs (dev-tools company, $80M ARR).
A PE firm offers to merge Vector with a portfolio company, Rune (observability,
$50M ARR), to create a combined platform. Product overlap is ~20%. Decision:
does the merger create more value than either standalone, where are the integration
risks, and what's the realistic combined ARR and margin in year 3?

### C15 — Due Diligence · Enterprise · Healthcare
**Client:** Ridgeline Capital (PE firm).
Considering a $900M acquisition of Camino Care (post-acute/home-health operator,
$650M revenue, 22% EBITDA). Revenue is 55% Medicare. A proposed CMS reimbursement
rule could cut rates 5%. Roll-up thesis assumes 8 tuck-in acquisitions. Decision:
is the thesis sound, what are the deal-breakers, and what's the defensible entry
valuation?

### C16 — Due Diligence · Mid-market · Manufacturing
**Client:** Anchor Industrial (strategic acquirer).
Evaluating acquisition of Delta Coatings ($120M revenue specialty-coatings maker).
Delta's margins look strong (24% EBITDA) but 60% of revenue comes from three
customers, and its largest product faces a REACH regulatory phase-out in 4 years.
Decision: is the quality of earnings real, what's the customer/regulatory risk, and

### C17 — Digital Transformation · Enterprise · Financial services
**Client:** Frontenac Insurance (P&C insurer, $5B premiums, 8,000 staff).
Claims processing takes 14 days vs. 3 for digital-native competitors. Core systems
are 30-year-old mainframe. Two prior transformation programs failed at a combined

### C18 — Digital Transformation · Public sector · Healthcare
**Client:** Regional Public Hospital Trust (5 hospitals, 900k catchment).
Digital maturity is low: paper records, no patient portal, fax-based referrals. A
government fund offers matched capital for digitization. Staff are change-fatigued and
clinically stretched. Decision: what to digitize first for patient-outcome and
efficiency impact, and how to sequence it without disrupting care.

### C19 — Org Redesign · Enterprise · Technology
**Client:** Aperture Systems (enterprise software, $1.2B revenue, 6,000 staff).
Growth by acquisition left 9 overlapping business units, 14 layers of management, and

centralized vs. federated functions) to speed decisions without breaking accountability.

### C20 — Org Redesign · Mid-market · Consumer goods
**Client:** Harbor & Vine (omnichannel home-goods retailer, $340M revenue).
Digital and store organizations operate as silos with separate P&Ls, causing channel

to stop the two channels from cannibalizing each other.

### C21 — Supply Chain · Enterprise · Manufacturing
**Client:** Ironwood Appliances ($4B revenue, global manufacturer).
A single-region component dependency caused $180M in lost sales during a recent

### C22 — Supply Chain · Mid-market · Consumer goods
**Client:** Petal & Stem (perishable flowers/plants e-commerce, $90M revenue).
Spoilage runs 12% of COGS; on-time delivery is 84%. Cold-chain logistics are

### C23 — Customer Experience · Enterprise · Financial services

### C24 — Customer Experience · Mid-market · Technology
**Client:** Cadence HR (HR SaaS, $45M ARR, SMB segment).
Gross churn is 22% annually (best-in-class ~10%). Onboarding takes 6 weeks; 40% of

### C25 — Product Strategy · Small · Technology
**Client:** Nomad Labs (developer-tools startup, $3M ARR, Series A).

### C26 — Product Strategy · Enterprise · Healthcare

### C27 — Growth Strategy · Small · Consumer goods

### C28 — Cost Reduction · Mid-market · Financial services

### C29 — Digital Transformation · Mid-market · Manufacturing

## Part 2 — Scoring Rubric (13 criteria, 0–10)

Each criterion is scored 0–10 with written justification per case. Anchors:

| Band | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 9–10 | Partner-quality. Would survive scrutiny at a top-tier firm. |
| 6–8 | Solid consultant-quality. Minor gaps a manager would coach. |
| 3–5 | Junior/flawed. Real gaps that would be sent back for rework. |
| 0–2 | Failing. Wrong, missing, or fabricated. |

### Criteria definitions

1. **Problem Understanding** — Did it identify the *real* decision, not the symptom?
   Distinguish presenting problem from underlying question. (9–10: reframes correctly
   and surfaces the true decision; 3–5: solves the stated symptom only.)

2. **Framework Selection** — Right framework(s) for the archetype, adapted not
   force-fit. (9–10: fit + explicit adaptation; 3–5: generic framework pasted on.)

3. **MECE Structure** — Issue tree is mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive,
   with owned/testable leaves. (9–10: clean, no gaps/overlaps; 3–5: overlapping or
   incomplete branches.)

4. **Evidence Usage** — Claims cite facts or labeled assumptions; no unsupported
   numbers. (9–10: every number traced; 3–5: some floating claims; 0–2: fabricated
   figures presented as fact.)

5. **Strategic Reasoning** — Quality of the logic connecting analysis to conclusion.
   (9–10: rigorous, non-obvious insight; 3–5: shallow/obvious.)

6. **Financial Reasoning** — Correct, appropriate quantitative work (bridges, unit
   economics, valuation, sensitivity). (9–10: correct + sensitivity-tested; 3–5:
   arithmetic gaps or missing where clearly needed; N/A allowed for soft cases with
   justification.)

7. **Risk Analysis** — Identifies real risks, quantifies where possible, and models
   competitive/regulatory response. (9–10: quantified register + counter-moves; 3–5:
   generic risk list.)

8. **Recommendation Quality** — Clear, decisive, defensible answer to the real
   question. (9–10: unambiguous with conditions; 3–5: hedged/vague "it depends.")

9. **Implementation Practicality** — Concrete, sequenced, feasible next steps.
   (9–10: sequenced with dependencies; 3–5: aspirational bullet list.)

10. **Report Quality** — Executive-readable structure, clarity, no filler.
    (9–10: tight, top-of-doc answer; 3–5: rambling or buried recommendation.)

11. **Traceability** — Every claim traces to evidence/assumption; `[ASSUMPTION]`
    labels survive to the report. (9–10: fully auditable; 3–5: labels lost in
    synthesis.)

12. **Determinism** — Same input → same output. **Note:** LLM-orchestrated
    engagements are inherently non-deterministic; this criterion is scored on the
    *deterministic layer* (rendering, validation, gates) and on whether the
    reasoning is *stable/reproducible in structure*, not byte-identical. Documented
    limitation, not a defect the system can score away.

13. **Overall Consulting Quality** — Holistic partner judgment: would you put this in
    front of the client? (Not a mean of the above — a gestalt.)

### Scoring protocol

- Execution and scoring are separated: the system runs the engagement faithfully
  (no manual improvement of outputs); scoring is a distinct critical pass.
- Every criterion gets a numeric score **and** a one–two sentence justification
  grounded in the actual artifact.
- Failures (hallucination, unsupported claim, framework misfit, governance bypass)
  are logged verbatim with the offending text.

## Part 3 — Execution Notes (integrity constraints)

- **No batch harness exists.** [Verified] Each engagement is a real multi-subagent
  LLM session via `/solve-case`. Metrics like process "memory usage" and code
  "latency" are not attributable to the LLM layer; performance is reported on the
  deterministic Python layer (retrieval, render, validation) where it is real.
- **Pilot scope:** a representative subset of cases is executed genuinely; results
  are explicitly labeled as pilot-based and not extrapolated beyond what was run.
- **Self-grading caveat:** because StratAgent's subagents are LLM prompts, the
  evaluator and the executor share a model family. Scoring is done as an adversarial
  critical pass to mitigate this, and the limitation is disclosed in the verdict.
